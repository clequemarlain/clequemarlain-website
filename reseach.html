Research interests

My work is motivated by the increasing decentralization of computation and services across edge, fog and cloud infrastructures. In such systems, agents compete for limited resources and their strategic decisions impact both efficiency and fairness. I study these problems through the lens of non-cooperative games and online learning.

Proportional allocation and the Kelly mechanism

A central object in my research is the Kelly mechanism, a proportional allocation rule where users submit bids and receive a share of the resource proportional to their bid. I investigate extensions of this mechanism under budget constraints and α-fair utility functions (α = 0, 1, 2, ...), with the aim of characterising existence and uniqueness of Nash equilibria, as well as the efficiency of the resulting allocations.

Learning dynamics in games

Beyond equilibrium analysis, I am particularly interested in how agents learn to play in repeated interactions. I study the convergence of different learning rules — such as synchronous best-response dynamics, Online Gradient Descent (OGD), Dual Averaging (quadratic and entropic) and extra-gradient methods — when they are applied to proportional allocation games with budgets.

I analyse both the convergence in actions (bids) and the convergence in utilities, and I study metrics such as distance to Nash equilibrium, price of anarchy and loss of social welfare. I am also interested in heterogeneous populations where different groups of agents run different learning algorithms contemporaneously.

Edge and fog computing applications

In the context of CoGNETs, I consider scenarios where edge resources are offered by infrastructure providers (InPs) and shared among mobile virtual network operators (MVNOs) or application providers. The goal is to design pricing and bidding policies that are both practically implementable and grounded in rigorous game-theoretic analysis.

Simulation tools

To support the theoretical analysis, I develop a simulation framework in Python/PyTorch for resource allocation games. This framework implements multiple learning dynamics, tracks convergence metrics, and produces visualisations (time series, phase plots, efficiency/fairness trade-offs). It is designed to be extensible and reproducible, and forms part of the research software developed for CoGNETs WP3.
